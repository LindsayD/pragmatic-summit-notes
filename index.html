The Shifting Bottleneck

The bottlenecks to building software are constantly moving. Code production used to be the constraint. Now, the bottleneck has shifted to review and validation. Next, it will move to gathering user requirements as quickly and accurately as possible.

One emerging pattern: run QA in a loop and flag regressions automatically. AI-assisted development is just one more level of abstraction â€” it still requires skilled people to control it.

Intellisense was "developer crack" and fundamentally changed how we work. But engineering as a job survived that transition, and it will survive this one too.

New Engineers, New Mindset

New-career engineers will be native to AI-assisted workflows. While they lack experience, they're also unbound by preconceptions about process or standards. For them, anything is possible.

The pace of change is staggering.

Two years is too far out to predict. Even six months is hard to predict.

From Debugging to Diagnosing

We will shift toward identifying quality by externally observing symptoms of things not working right, rather than debugging code internals.

This feels like diagnosing a patient instead of programming. Doctors only order blood tests and exploratory surgery when symptoms are vague or haven't already been identified and categorized.

How Our Work Is Changing

The bottleneck in software development is migrating steadily upward: from writing code, to reviewing and validating it, to gathering user requirements as fast and accurately as possible. The most valuable engineering skill is increasingly about understanding what to build rather than how to build it.

Kent Beck captured this shift most starkly: the satisfaction of getting one function right "just doesn't matter anymore." Coming from the person who championed TDD and extreme programming, this signals a fundamental reorientation â€” away from craft at the code level and toward mastery of the problem domain.

We're also moving back toward "solo programming," but in a new form: one engineer directing many agents. The social dynamics of engineering teams may change significantly, with less need for the coordination structures that Agile was built around.

This is actually comforting for many people who tend to be introverts, who hated the open office and enjoyed diving deep into code by themselves. But it raises real questions about how we maintain shared understanding and mentorship without the old collaboration structures.

Practices We Can Adopt Now

Defect Wednesday: File a ticket for a non-urgent defect every week. Teams build the habit of spotting polish and quality issues, and develop a rhythm of fixing them â€” improving the product experience beyond just critical bugs. (Tuomas Artman, Linear)

Rotating Quality Engineer: One developer focuses on finding bugs and small fixes for a week, then rotates. (Michelle Lim, Flint)

Features/Futures Rhythm: Deliberately alternate between shipping features and investing in the foundation â€” paying down tech debt, learning new tools, gathering requirements. It's hard to see futures and easy to see features, so this requires intentional discipline. (Kent Beck)

Raise Test Coverage Aggressively: The economics of testing have flipped. The old trade-off of high vs "just enough" coverage was always about how long it took to write and run tests. AI collapses that cost. The important thing now is verifying that tests are good and actually reflect desired outcomes. TDD is likely the right path forward.

DevEx matters more than ever â€” just call it "Agent Experience" now. Fast CI, good documentation, clean APIs, well-architected code. All things that would have made life easier for humans but were never approved for time and resource expenditure. Now the agents need them, so their cost will be justified â€” and we can use agents to build and maintain them too.

What Leaders and Organizations Should Watch

AI is an amplifier, not a solution. Some companies see 2Ã— more customer-facing incidents while others see 50% fewer. The difference isn't the tools â€” it's the organizational foundations underneath. Fix your foundations before scaling AI adoption.

Adoption does not equal impact. 80% of organizations have investigated general-purpose LLMs, but only 40% have successfully implemented them. For embedded or task-specific GenAI, that number drops to 5%. Tools primarily enhance individual productivity; organizational gains require organizational-level strategy.

Onboarding time is being cut in half. This has significant implications for team composition, hiring velocity, and how quickly new engineers can contribute meaningfully.

Security risks are real and underappreciated. Martin Fowler specifically warned about security incidents from giving AI too much access â€” like letting it read emails. As agents gain more autonomy, the attack surface grows.

Productivity metrics are "doubly bogus" now. It's not about code volume or PR count â€” it's about customer outcomes. As Kent Beck quipped: "How many PRs do you want? I will make that many."

Looking Ahead

Models will get better at consuming and following design systems and standards. Investing in well-defined architectural artifacts now will have compounding returns as AI tools improve.

The concept of a "self-evolving codebase" â€” where AI continuously refactors and improves the code it works on â€” may move from idea to reality faster than we expect.
